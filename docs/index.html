Polynomial Root Finder — Educational Demo (G–K Method)

Mathematical Background and Method Description

We consider a real polynomial

p(x) = anxn + an−1xn−1 + ··· + a1x + a0,

of degree n ≥ 5, where all coefficients ai ∈ ℝ. The objective of this platform is to illustrate an exact and reliable numerical procedure for computing the positive real roots of p(x) = 0.

For polynomial equations of degree greater than four, no general closed-form solutions exist. Root computation therefore relies on numerical methods. The approach presented here combines:

  • theoretical localization of roots,
  • rigorous root existence verification,
  • and a convergent iterative scheme based on the G–K decomposition.

1. Localization of Positive Roots (Cauchy Bound)

An upper bound M for all positive real roots of p(x) is obtained using Cauchy’s theorem:

x ≤ M = 1 + max0 ≤ i < n |ai / an|.

Hence, all positive real roots lie in the interval (0, M).

2. Root Existence and Counting (Sturm Theorem)

To determine whether roots actually exist in the interval (0, M), Sturm’s theorem is applied. By constructing the Sturm sequence associated with p(x) and evaluating sign variations at the interval endpoints, the exact number of real roots in (0, M) is established.

This step guarantees that the numerical iteration is applied only on intervals where roots are known to exist.

3. G–K Decomposition

The polynomial is rewritten in the form:

p(x) = G(x) − K(x),

where G(x) > 0 and K(x) > 0 on (0, M). This decomposition allows the construction of a fixed-point formulation suited for iterative computation.

4. G–K Fixed-Point Iteration

5. Convergence Analysis and Theoretical Justification

The convergence of the G–K method is based on a constructive decomposition of the polynomial

P(x) = G(x) − K(x),

where the functions G(x) and K(x) are positive and strictly increasing on the interval (0, M). Such a decomposition can be obtained by separating the positive and negative coefficient parts of P(x) and introducing an auxiliary linear function m(x) = Lx, with L > 0 sufficiently large.

A positive real root x* of P(x) = 0 satisfies

G(x*) = K(x*),

and is therefore a fixed point of the iterative scheme defined by

K(xn+1) = G(xn).

When the auxiliary function is chosen as m(x) = Lx, the iteration can be written explicitly as

xn+1 = xn + P(xn) / L.

Since both G and K are strictly increasing on (0, M), the iteration preserves order and generates a monotone sequence. For any initial value x0 ∈ (0, M), the sequence (xn) converges to the positive real root x*.

The convergence is linear and depends explicitly on the choice of the parameter L. Larger values of L improve robustness and monotonicity, while smaller values may accelerate convergence at the expense of stability. This trade-off explains the reliable but conservative behavior of the G–K method compared with Newton-type iterations.

From the G–K decomposition, an iteration of the form

xk+1 = Φ(xk)

is obtained. Under appropriate conditions, this sequence converges monotonically to a positive real root of p(x).
